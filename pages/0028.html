Sophia Hoare’s month-long tour of south Wales in July-August 1809,1with her husband Henry, reads as a trip made mainly out of curiosity and for pleasure. Leaving London on the 7thJuly, they travelled to the Wye Valley via Gloucester, taking a boat trip down the Wye to Monmouth in a passage that includes a description of Tintern Abbey towards evening (ff. 26r-26v). A disappointing expedition to Llantony Abbey follows, in which Hoare finds herself unimpressed with the (ultimately abandoned) efforts of the poet Walter Savage Landor to develop the site.2

From there, the Hoares turned west towards Carmarthenshire and Pembrokeshire, visiting the dramatically-set Carreg Cennen Castle (fig.1) and (by candlelight) its underground tunnels, and the small cathedral city of St David’s. Their return via the south coast took them via castles and country houses, and to the spectacular cliffside chapel of St Govan’s – a medieval pilgrimage site set in a limestone gorge. Both Hoare and her husband sketched on this trip; her interest in contemporary art is also revealed by her attempt to find the exact point from which Turner had recently portrayed Pembroke Castle (f. 33v). The final stretch of the journey took them through the rapidly changing central and eastern coastal regions, where Hoare recorded both the beauty of the landscape and the damaging environmental effects of the new heavy industries: ‘The Vale of Glamorgan would be beautiful but for the Copper and Iron Works the Smoke of which is very unpleasant and so thick one can hardly see beyond it’ (f. 41v).

Note on the text:

The source text is a small notebook, written in Sophia Hoare’s legible hand. The original manuscript is unpaginated; folio numbers have been added to this edition.
