For the Welsh-born writer Hester Piozzi (1741-1821; fig. 1), the 1780s was a decade of transition in personal and creative terms. Her first husband, the wealthy brewer Henry Thrale, died in 1781; amid considerable controversy she married her second, Gabriel Piozzi, in 1784. Her life as Hester Thrale had been shaped first by motherhood and then, partly through the influence of Samuel Johnson (a frequent presence in the Thrale households from 1765 onwards), by her roles as a literary hostess and prolific diarist. Much of London society, and particularly Piozzi’s Bluestocking friends and acquaintances, felt there was something scandalous in her marriage to Gabriel Piozzi, an Italian Catholic singer and music master who had once taught her daughters. Rumours circulated that she had married for lust, and that for his part Gabriel Piozzi was only interested in her money.1 The marriage resulted in lasting estrangement from close former friends such as Frances Burney and even Samuel Johnson.

Her new life as Hester Piozzi brought her a certain amount of financial independence, along with a newfound hostility towards England; Italy and Wales would grow to be more important to her as a result. It also gave rise to a writing career that would see Piozzi become a hugely innovative author of nonfictional prose. The death of Johnson in 1784 prompted her to publish first Anecdotes of the Late Samuel Johnson (1786) and then Letters to and from the Late Samuel Johnson (1788), two commercially successful, if controversial, works that established her as a literary figure in her own right. The extended continental honeymoon the Piozzis had undertaken between 1784 and 1787, during which she produced the Johnson biography, also yielded a major work of travel writing, Observations and Reflections Made in the Course of a Journey Through France, Italy, and Germany (1789). All three of these books reflected the anecdotal style with which she became identified, and which would also feature in her later groundbreaking works on synonymy and history.

In the summer of 1789, Piozzi, in the company of her husband and youngest daughter, made a tour of Scotland, travelling up through York and Durham to Edinburgh, across to Glasgow and on to the Highlands, before returning down the coast through Carlisle and Liverpool to north-east Wales. Piozzi had embarked on this tour at virtually the same moment that Observations and Reflections was being published and may have had her Italian travels of some five years earlier much in mind as a result. Continental comparisons abound in the record she kept of the Scottish tour: the countryside around Whitby is ‘coarse as Westphalia’ (f. 4r); she records grapes ‘growing wild as if at Florence or Genoa’ (f. 5v) near Durham; on crossing the Scottish border, where the country ‘changes its Appearance gradually, and the manners fade away visibly’, she is reminded of ‘Vanvitelli’s Aqueduct at Caserta’ (f. 7r). Edinburgh calls to mind Paris – the city’s poor women ‘have much of the French Manner’ (f. 7v) – and Glasgow is ‘wholly like a Continental Town’ (f. 11r).

For all its personal or anecdotal style, Piozzi’s Observations and Reflections shows her engaging in serious reportage, commenting for example on the national condition of France in the mid-1780s, and on topics such as commerce, trade, government and society. Different impulses frame the Scottish tour, which, despite the immediate political context of July 1789, makes only glancing references to contemporary events (see for example her comments on ‘the Curse of Despotic Governments’ at f. 3r). By contrast, Piozzi’s letters from Scotland offer an alternative narrative, written from a much more political perspective that rarely surfaces in the tour. These letters (and to a lesser extent her diary, Thraliana) write and rewrite multiple accounts of the same place: specific points and phrases echo across different manuscripts. On 21 July she describes the scene in sociable Edinburgh to her friend Samuel Lysons as one in which ‘everybody is talking of Famine and Bloodshed, and filling each other’s Heads … with the most tremendous Uproar’.3 That almost nothing of this turmoil makes it into the written tour perhaps reflects late eighteenth-century expectations of travel writing as detached and authoritative, rather than ephemeral and of the moment.

At least in theory, Piozzi was free to take any approach she chose in the Scottish tour because it remained unpublished (though in practice it may of course have been read in manuscript by a select audience). But why did she apparently have no plans to publish this work, especially given that her previous books had sold so well? Perhaps Johnson’s Scottish tour – which she explicitly mentions only once, at f. 10v – loomed before her, but Piozzi herself suggested that there were other reasons. Surviving correspondence shows that her Scottish excursion was surrounded by an element of ‘celebrity’ appeal. Her progress north was being followed in the World newspaper (edited by her acquaintance Charles Este): on 9 July 1789, the paper informed readers that ‘Mrs. Piozzi, who … writes so well of her travels, that we wish her to travel most of her life, is now about to enter Scotland’, while on 31 July came the announcement ‘Mrs. Piozzi is gone to Glasgow’.3

Piozzi knew that the World was reporting on her Scottish tour, and complained sharply to Sophia Byron, whom she believed had fed the information to Este. On her return, she confided the following thoughts to her diary:

When People press me to write my Tour of our own Island in good Time! they say it to ensnare me: was I to act according to such Advice I should deservedly lose the little Fame I have already acquired.4

Piozzi’s Scottish tour is a lively, wide-ranging and highly readable work. Her sense that publishing it would be detrimental is perhaps not easy for us to understand today, but it reflects the tight conditions in which women writers published (or did not) in certain genres – travel writing among them.
