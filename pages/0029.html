George Capper’s tour, made in May 1802, offers a view of south Wales through the eyes of a clergyman. The Cambridge-educated Capper (1767-1847) came from a Suffolk background, but was perhaps drawn to Wales for family reasons – in 1802 his uncle James Capper, an army officer and meteorologist who spent much of his career in India, was living in Cardiff [fig. 1], where this tour begins. Capper, who had become the rector of Little Blakenham near Ipswich in 1794, writes very much as a typical leisured tourist. He records antiquarian and geographical scenes of interest (castles, churches, monuments, ruins, bridges, canals), and notes the rapid industrial changes taking place in the south Wales valleys – including the vast wealth being accumulated by individuals such as Richard Crawshay, ironmaster at Cyfarthfa, the largest ironworks in Britain in the 1790s (f. 7r).

From Cardiff the tour moves west and north, to Merthyr and Pontypridd and then from Abergavenny to Brecon. Capper jots down comments on the quality of the food and accommodation, and their cost, while an incident involving a theft at Crickhowell (f. 11r) nicely illustrates the rapid turnover of guests at Welsh inns and the often crowded nature of the tourist experience. Suspecting that an Irish servant has stolen his watch, Capper is able to trace and follow the inn’s previous customers in a way that suggests the distinctiveness and visibility, rather than anonymity, of parties on the home tour.

Capper reflects on the layered nature of Welsh landscapes throughout his tour, balancing the ‘wearisome tract of barren Mountain’ (f. 8r) he finds at the heads of the valleys with thoughts of the mineral resources (read: riches) that lie beneath. Travelling westwards into the rural topography of Carmarthenshire, his attitudes towards landscape take on different resonances. Capper displays his knowledge of Wales in wider eighteenth-century culture through a visit to John Dyer’s Grongar Hill [fig. 2], but in this section the social commentary side of his tour takes a sharpened political turn. Capper’s opening paragraphs on Cardiff praise the virtues of refined retirement, but no such society seems to him to be possible in turn-of-the-century Carmarthenshire, where a decade of wartime and scarcity, alongside a genuine diffusion of revolutionary principles in Wales, has led to the tangible emergence of ‘That equalizing spirit … that has gone forth in the world’ (f. 17r). Instead, Capper imagines a near future in which the almost-feudal relationship between the Welsh people and local gentry families will be replaced by a modern one ‘rid … of all superfluous respect for their superiors’ (f. 17r). ‘I think great doubt may be entertain’d whether they will be either the better or the happier for their change of manners’, he concludes.

Note on the source text:

The manuscript source is written in George Capper’s generally clear hand, with relatively few emendations. The source text mostly occurs on recto pages, with the occasional authorial note on facing verso pages.
